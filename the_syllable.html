The syllable

Represents a level of structure intuitively recognized by speakers of the language; it figures importantly in the rhythm and prosody of the language.

Syllable structure:

The syllable is divided into two segments named: Onset and rime. Additionally, the rime consists of the vowel, which is treated as the nucleus, plus any following consonant(s), treated as the coda.



Onset  

The onset of a syllable consists of all the consonants that precede the nucleus.

Nucleus 

The nucleus of a syllable is a vowel.

Coda 

The coda of a syllable consists of all the consonants that are located after the nucleus.


Type of syllables:

Open syllables:

Syllables that have an onset and a nucleus, but no coda.



Closed syllables:

Syllables that include coda.
